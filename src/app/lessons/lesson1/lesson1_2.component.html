We will now speak about major scale. A scale is constructed using notes in a certain pattern, which can be described by tones and semitones. Before looking at the major scale pattern lets try to figure it out from the C major scale.

Here's how a C major scale looks and sounds like.


As it can be seen from the above figure, there is a semitone distance only between B & C, and between E and F.

Hence the major scale pattern looks like this: T-T-S-T-T-T-S (where T stands for Tone and S stands for Semitone)

The notes of the scale are known as degrees, the first being the 1st degree or the tonic, second being 2nd degree and so on.

Accordingly, the G major scale is as follows: G-A-B-C-D-E-F#-G

Next we come to intervals. The distance between any two notes is basically an interval.For example, in the following figure, the interval is a 5th, as the top note is 5 steps above (by stepping into the next space or line in succession above) from the bottom note.


Now a question might arise in your mind, as to whether the following figure is a fifth or not


Someone will say, clearly this is a different interval, right?? The answer is both yes and no. If we judge it from the 'quantity' or interval number alone, then it is a 5th. However, if we try to incorporate the 'quality' or the fact that the higher note is sharped, then it is somewhat 'a variation of' a fifth. Not to forget, if you try to play it on a keyboard it will sound completely different. Hence, defining a quality of an interval is as crucial as its quantity counterpart. We will look into this in later lessons.
