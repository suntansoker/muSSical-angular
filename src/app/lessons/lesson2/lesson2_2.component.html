This brings us to the concept of relative major and minor scales. A relative minor scale of a particular major scale is constructed in the following manner: Count down three half steps or semitones from the tonic of the major scale and bingo! You get the tonic of the relative minor scale.

So Counting down 3 half steps below C (B-A#-A) brings us to A and hence A minor scale is the relative minor scale of C major. Cool, right?!?

Typically major is not associated with 5ths and this is particularly what is known as a perfect fifth. It consists of seven semitones in between the notes. A semitone less will make it a diminished fifth.

Majors and minors are generally associated with 2nd, 3rd, 6th and 7th.
On the other hand, perfect and diminished are associated with 4th, 5th and octaves.

A simplest way to know the various major, and perfect intervals is to compare it with the C Major Scale. Notes in C major scale are at major or perfect intervals from the root note C. For ex: C to D is a major 2nd, C to E is a major 3rd, C to A is major 6th and so on. From this note intervals we can get the number of half steps present in a particular interval. A minor version of it will be mere 1 semitone or half-step less than that.
